Why Might My Child Need an Evaluation?

Understanding the signs and reasons for seeking psychological or educational testing.

Deciding to pursue psychological or educational testing for your child can be a significant step. Often, parents, caregivers, or educators consider evaluations when they observe persistent difficulties that impact a child's learning, behavior, social interactions, or emotional well-being. An evaluation can provide clarity, identify underlying causes, and guide supportive interventions.

Common Reasons to Consider an Evaluation:

Academic Difficulties:

  • Struggling to keep up with grade-level expectations in reading, writing, or math.
  • Significant difficulty with homework completion or organization.
  • Poor performance on tests despite apparent understanding of material.
  • Teacher expressing concerns about academic progress or learning style.
  • Suspected dyslexia, dysgraphia, dyscalculia, or other specific learning disabilities.

Attention and Behavioral Concerns:

  • Difficulty paying attention, staying focused, or easily distracted.
  • Hyperactivity, impulsivity, or restlessness that is disruptive.
  • Challenges with organization, planning, and time management (executive functions).
  • Frequent behavioral outbursts, defiance, or difficulty following rules.
  • Social difficulties, such as trouble making or keeping friends.
  • Possible ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder).

Developmental Concerns:

  • Delays in reaching developmental milestones (language, motor skills, social skills).
  • Unusual patterns of behavior, interests, or communication.
  • Difficulties with social interaction, understanding social cues, or repetitive behaviors.
  • Suspected Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD).

Emotional Difficulties:

  • Persistent sadness, irritability, or loss of interest in activities.
  • Excessive worry, anxiety, fears, or phobias.
  • Significant mood swings or emotional reactivity.
  • Withdrawal from social activities or family.
  • Changes in sleep or appetite.

Neurological or Medical Factors:

  • History of concussion, traumatic brain injury (TBI), or other neurological conditions.
  • Medical conditions that may affect cognitive or emotional functioning (e.g., epilepsy, genetic disorders).
  • Changes in cognitive abilities following an illness or injury.

How an Evaluation Can Help

A comprehensive evaluation aims to:

  • Identify Strengths and Weaknesses: Understand your child's unique profile.
  • Provide Diagnostic Clarity: Determine if a specific condition (like ADHD, ASD, or a learning disability) is present.
  • Guide Interventions: Offer tailored recommendations for school, home, and therapy.
  • Facilitate Accommodations: Provide documentation for IEPs, 504 plans, or other support services.
  • Empower Families: Equip you with knowledge and strategies to support your child effectively.

If you recognize some of these signs in your child and are wondering if an evaluation is appropriate, we encourage you to contact us for a consultation. We can discuss your concerns and help determine the best path forward.
